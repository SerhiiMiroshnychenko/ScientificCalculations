## Response to Reviewer 3 (Round 3)

Dear Reviewer,

We sincerely thank you for the third round of review and for your positive overall assessment that the manuscript has been improved and that most of the previous comments have been properly taken into account. We are grateful for the three remaining minor corrections, which we have implemented in full. Below we provide a point-by-point response; for each item we indicate the specific changes introduced in the manuscript.

### Comment 1
> No need to write "the following contributions to the body of scientific knowledge". Shorter variant as "the following contribution" is enough. In my comments I accented "to the body of scientific knowledge" to clearer explain what I mean.

**Response 1.** Thank you for this clarification. We understand that the phrase "to the body of scientific knowledge" was used in your earlier comments only to explain your intent and was not meant to be reproduced verbatim in the manuscript. We have therefore shortened the wording accordingly. We retained the plural form "contributions" because three distinct contributions (C1–C3) are itemized at that point; only the qualifier has been removed.

**Changes in manuscript.**
- *Introduction* (Section 1): the lead-in to the contributions block now reads "To address problems P1–P3, this study makes the following contributions:", with the phrase "to the body of scientific knowledge" removed.

### Comment 2
> My comment of numbered equations is still valid. *"There are numbered Eqs.(1)-(3), which are not explicitly used (with referencing). Please, show where and how the equations are applied."* Yes, text references were added. Such an equation must be referenced not when the equation is introduced, but when the equation is used for constructing some results useful for this paper research. If some equation is not used then the equation is not needed to represent in the paper.

**Response 2.** We fully accept this principle and have applied it strictly: an equation is retained only if it is used to construct a result reported in the paper, and it is now referenced at the point of that use rather than at the point of its introduction. Re-examining the three equations under this rule:

- The **classical Borda score** (former Eq. (2)) is *not* used to construct any result; it appeared only as a contrast to motivate the proposed rule. In accordance with your guidance ("if some equation is not used then the equation is not needed"), we **removed it as a numbered display equation** and now state its sum-of-positions formula inline, within the sentence that motivates the modification.
- The **rank definition** (Eq. (1)) and the **modified Borda mean rank** (former Eq. (3), now renumbered **Eq. (2)**) *are* used to construct the reported results, and they are now referenced exactly where those results are built: each cell of the Fig. 8 heatmap reports the per-method rank computed with Eq. (1), and the "Average Rank" column reports the consolidated mean rank computed with Eq. (2), which in turn determines the "Overall Rank" and the ordering of Table 1.

**Changes in manuscript** (Sections 3 and 4).
- Removed the classical Borda display equation (former Eq. (2)); its sum-of-positions formula B(s_i) = sum_{j=1..k} (n - tau_j(s_i) + 1) is now given inline in the prose of Section 3.
- Renumbered the modified Borda mean-rank equation from Eq. (3) to **Eq. (2)**; all cross-references were updated accordingly.
- Removed the sentence "Eq. (2) is reproduced here only as the classical reference formulation against which the proposed modification is contrasted", which is no longer applicable.
- Added the explicit point-of-use references where the results are constructed: in the description of Fig. 8 / Table 1, the cell ranks are stated to be obtained from Eq. (1) and the "Average Rank" column from Eq. (2), tying both equations directly to the reported ordering.
- Updated the Stage III description accompanying Fig. 7 to read "using Eq. (1)" and "using Eq. (2)", with the classical sum-of-positions Borda score now described in words rather than as a numbered equation.

After these changes the manuscript contains two numbered equations, Eq. (1) and Eq. (2), both of which are explicitly referenced at the point where they generate the results in Table 1 and Fig. 8.

### Comment 3
> Figs. 9–13 are low-informative. They look like demonstration, not like analytics. I recommend augment the figures with more analytical information (e.g., parameters, statistics, metrics). So more effectively use the space that each figure covers.

**Response 3.** We agree and have converted Figs. 9–13 from purely illustrative plots into analytical ones. Each figure now carries a compact statistics panel placed in the previously empty area of the plot, and the key metric of each figure is also reported concisely in the text. The data points, marker sizes, colour coding, and legends are unchanged, so the figures remain directly comparable with the previous version while now conveying quantitative evidence rather than only a visual trend.

**Changes in manuscript** (Section 4).
- Each of Figs. 9–13 now includes an in-figure statistics box reporting, as appropriate: the per-bin sample size n; 95% Wilson confidence intervals for the success rate of each bin; Spearman's rho with its p-value; the point-biserial correlation r; and a goodness-of-fit metric — the linear R^2 for the monotonic relationships (Figs. 9 and 10) and the quadratic R^2 for the non-monotonic order_amount relationship (Fig. 11). For Fig. 12 the box additionally reports a two-proportion z-test for discount presence, and for Fig. 13 a chi-square test of independence with the Cramer's V effect size.
- The accompanying text now states the principal statistic of each figure concisely, since the remaining detail can be read directly from the figure:
  - **Fig. 9 (messages):** significant positive monotonic association, Spearman's rho = 0.45 (p < 0.001), well approximated by a linear trend across 20 equal-frequency bins (R^2 = 0.81), with narrow per-bin 95% confidence intervals (<= +/- 1.5 pp; n = 86,794).
  - **Fig. 10 (changes):** Spearman's rho = 0.24 (p < 0.001), approximately linear (+6.4 pp per bin, R^2 = 0.64), success rising from 45.9% to 86.3%.
  - **Fig. 11 (order_amount):** non-monotonic (Spearman's rho = -0.15) with a quadratic fit R^2 = 0.74 and a peak success rate of 84.2% in the 710–789 USD bin (n = 67,242), which explains the divergence of single-method rankings on this feature.
  - **Fig. 12 (discount):** the difference between discounted (73.0%, n = 89) and non-discounted (63.1%) orders is not statistically significant (two-proportion z-test, p = 0.052; Cramer's V = 0.007), and the 95% confidence intervals within the small discounted subset are very wide, confirming it is underpowered.
  - **Fig. 13 (day of week):** a statistically detectable but practically negligible effect (chi-square(6) = 51.2, p < 0.001; Cramer's V = 0.02; range 61.3%–72.1%), with the higher weekend values resting on small samples (Saturday n = 139, Sunday n = 122).
We once again thank you for your careful and constructive review. We believe that removing the unused equation, anchoring the remaining equations to the results they generate, and upgrading Figs. 9–13 into analytical figures have further improved the rigor, transparency, and clarity of the manuscript.

With respect,
The Authors
