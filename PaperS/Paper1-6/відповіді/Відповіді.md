## Response to Reviewer 3 (Round 3)

Dear Reviewer,

We sincerely thank you for the third-round review of our manuscript, "A Multi-Algorithmic Method for Ranking B2B Order Success Factors in an ERP Environment," and for acknowledging that the manuscript has improved after the previous revision. We appreciate your remaining comments, which helped us further improve the conciseness, methodological transparency, and analytical value of the presentation. Below we provide a point-by-point response.

### Comment 1
> No need to write "the following contributions to the body of scientific knowledge". Shorter variant as "the following contribution" is enough. In my comments I accented "to the body of scientific knowledge" to clearer explain what I mean.

**Response 1.** Thank you for this clarification. We agree that the previous wording was unnecessarily long. In the revised manuscript, we shortened this phrase and removed the expression "to the body of scientific knowledge" from the corresponding sentence.

**Changes in manuscript.**
- In the Introduction, the sentence now reads: "To address problems P1--P3, this study makes the following contributions:"
- The contribution list C1--C3 was retained, but the introductory phrase was made more concise in accordance with the reviewer's recommendation.

### Comment 2
> My comment of numbered equations is still valid. There are numbered Eqs.(1)-(3), which are not explicitly used (with referencing). Please, show where and how the equations are applied. Yes, text references were added. Such an equation must be referenced not when the equation is introduced, but when the equation is used for constructing some results useful for this paper research. If some equation is not used then the equation is not needed to represent in the paper.

**Response 2.** Thank you for this important clarification. We agree that the numbered equations should be referenced only where they are actually used to construct the research results, not merely where they are introduced. We therefore revised the equation block and the corresponding explanatory text.

The classical Borda formula was removed as a numbered equation, because it served only as a reference formulation and was not directly used in the calculations. As a result, only the two equations actually used in the proposed procedure remain numbered: Eq. (1), which transforms scorer-specific feature scores into ranks, and Eq. (2), which aggregates these ranks through the modified Borda mean-rank rule.

**Changes in manuscript.**
- Removed the previously numbered classical Borda formula.
- Renumbered the modified Borda mean-rank equation as Eq. (2).
- Revised the methodological explanation to state that the consolidated ordering is obtained by applying Eq. (1) to each feature-scorer pair and then aggregating the resulting per-scorer ranks via Eq. (2).
- Revised Stage III of Fig. 7 description: per-scorer scores are transformed into ranks using Eq. (1), and these ranks are aggregated into the consolidated ordering using Eq. (2).
- Revised the Results section to explicitly state that Table 1 scores are transformed into per-scorer ranks via Eq. (1) and consolidated through Eq. (2) into the ordering visualized in Fig. 8.
- Added an explicit sentence after Fig. 8 explaining that Fig. 8 is the direct empirical application of Eqs. (1) and (2): the scorer-specific ranks are obtained from Table 1 using Eq. (1), while the "Average Rank" and "Overall Rank" fields are obtained using Eq. (2).

### Comment 3
> Figs. 9-13 are low-informative. They look like demonstration, not like analytics. I recommend augment the figures with more analytical information (e.g., parameters, statistics, metrics). So more effectively use the space that each figure covers.

**Response 3.** Thank you for this helpful recommendation. We agree that the previous versions of Figs. 9--13 were primarily illustrative and did not use the available visual space effectively enough for analytical interpretation. We therefore regenerated these figures and augmented them with statistical information.

The revised figures now include statistical summary boxes and additional analytical information directly connected with the plotted relationships.

**Changes in manuscript and figures.**
- Regenerated Figs. 9--13 with statistical summary boxes.
- Added bin or category sample sizes and 95% Wilson confidence intervals for success rates.
- Added association metrics relevant to the type of predictor:
  - Fig. 9 (message count): Spearman rho, point-biserial correlation, weighted linear trend, and R².
  - Fig. 10 (changes count): Spearman rho, point-biserial correlation, weighted linear trend, and R².
  - Fig. 11 (order amount): Spearman rho, point-biserial correlation, and quadratic-fit R² to reflect the nonlinear inverted-U relationship.
  - Fig. 12 (discount): two-proportion test for discount presence, Cramer's V, positive-discount subset size, and Wilson confidence intervals.
  - Fig. 13 (day of week): chi-square test, Cramer's V, category sample sizes, and Wilson confidence intervals.
- Revised the Results section text accompanying Figs. 9--13 to interpret these metrics explicitly rather than only describing visual patterns.
- Clarified that the positive-discount subset contains only 89 orders, so the observed within-discount pattern should be interpreted as exploratory rather than confirmatory.
- Updated the figure captions to indicate that the figures now include statistical summaries.

We believe these revisions directly address the reviewer's concern: the figures now provide analytical parameters, uncertainty information, and association metrics, while the surrounding text explains how these results support the interpretation of the selected predictors.

Once again, we sincerely thank you for your constructive review and for the precise clarification of the remaining issues. We believe that the latest revisions have improved the manuscript's conciseness, methodological clarity, and analytical presentation.

With respect,
The Authors
