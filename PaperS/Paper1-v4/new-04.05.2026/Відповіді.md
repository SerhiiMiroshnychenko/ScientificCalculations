## Response to Reviewer 1
Dear Reviewer,
We sincerely thank you for your careful reading of our manuscript, “Ranking B2B Order Success Factors in ERP Systems: A Multi-Algorithmic Approach,” and for your detailed and constructive comments. We highly appreciate your positive assessment of the originality and relevance of the study. Your remarks have been very valuable for improving the clarity, structure, and presentation of the manuscript. Below we provide a point-by-point response.
Remark 1
It is unclear why “ERP systems” are mentioned in the title, since ranking the success factors of B2B customer orders may be considered a broader problem not directly related to ERP-specific components or characteristics.
Response 1
Thank you for this important remark. We agree that the problem of identifying B2B order success factors can be considered in a broader context than ERP systems alone. In our study, however, the empirical data, the selected feature set, and the analytical setting are directly derived from an ERP-based transactional environment, namely the Odoo ERP system. The analyzed variables represent operational, transactional, customer-related, communication-related, and temporal information extracted from ERP-based business process records.
To eliminate the ambiguity noted by the reviewer, we revised the manuscript title to: “A Multi-Algorithmic Method for Ranking B2B Order Success Factors in an ERP Environment.” This new formulation is more precise because it presents ERP not as a claim about universally ERP-specific factors, but as the empirical environment in which the transactions were recorded, the variables were operationalized, and the analysis was conducted.
This ERP context is additionally justified by the practical implementation objective of the study. As noted in the manuscript, the research is directly connected with the development of an enhanced forecasting module for the Odoo ERP platform (Miroshnychenko, 2024). In this respect, the revised title more accurately reflects both the source of the data and the intended application context of the proposed ranking method.
Remark 2
Figures 1–5 are not sufficiently informative, and the color intensity is not associated with a quantitative value. Perhaps tables would be better.
Response 2
Thank you for this useful observation. We agree that the original visual format of Figures 1–5 did not provide the clearest representation of the distributions of the analyzed variables. In the revised manuscript, these figures were replaced with violin plots, which more informatively display the distributional properties of the selected features across successful and unsuccessful orders.
We did not replace the figures with tables, because preserving a graphical representation helps the reader better perceive the structure, spread, and asymmetry of the data. To strengthen the quantitative interpretation, we added explicit numerical descriptions of the corresponding statistical characteristics in the text. In this way, the revised version combines visual clarity with quantitative explanation.
Remark 3
Figure 6 does not explain how qualitatively different variables from Stage III-a, Stage III-b, and Stage III-c are combined into the result (“Prepared data”).
Response 3
Thank you for identifying this lack of clarity. We revised the methodological explanation associated with Figure 6 to state more explicitly that the outputs of Stage III-a, Stage III-b, and Stage III-c are combined within the same order-level table by applying the corresponding type-specific transformations to the relevant columns, thereby forming the final analytical feature matrix (“Prepared data”).
More specifically, temporal variables are transformed using temporal feature extraction and cyclical encoding where appropriate; categorical variables are encoded using the selected compact one-hot encoding procedure; and numerical variables are scaled using robust preprocessing methods. These transformations are applied directly to the same order-record dataset, so that the original row structure is preserved while raw variables are replaced by their processed representations, which then serve as the common input for the feature-significance evaluation methods. This logic has been clarified in the methodological description.
Remark 4
The “Features” column in Table 1 (and Fig. 8) contains probably database field names, not names of importance metrics.
Response 4
Thank you for this remark. We would like to clarify that the entries shown in the “Technical Feature Name” column of Table 1 and on the y-axis of Fig. 8 are technical names of the analyzed features, not names of importance metrics. These names were retained intentionally to preserve consistency with the analytical dataset and to ensure transparency and reproducibility of the computational workflow.
We nevertheless agree that this presentation may be insufficiently transparent for a broader audience. Therefore, in the revised manuscript we clarified this point more explicitly by renaming the first column of Table 1 as “Technical Feature Name” and by adding Appendix A – Feature Name Mapping, which provides the substantive meaning, category, data type, and measurement scale of the analyzed variables.
Remark 5
The contents of the cells of Table 1 are not discussed in the text.
Response 5
Thank you. We agree that the discussion of Table 1 required further elaboration. In the revised manuscript, we expanded the Results section to discuss selected values presented in Table 1 more explicitly.
More specifically, we added a concise numerical paragraph highlighting representative entries from the table, including the consistently high values of order_messages, the cross-method variation observed for order_amount and partner_success_rate, and the near-baseline or zero values associated with discount_total and the fine-grained temporal features. This revision makes the contents of Table 1 more directly visible in the text while preserving the concise structure of the Results section.
Remark 6
The font in Fig. 8 is too small, and it is not explained what the numbers in the cells mean.
Response 6
Thank you for this helpful remark. We revised Fig. 8 to improve readability by increasing the font size and refining the overall visual presentation. In addition, we explicitly clarified in the manuscript that the numbers in the heatmap cells correspond to the ranks assigned by the individual methods, that “Average Rank” denotes the mean of these method-specific ranks, and that “Overall Rank” represents the final aggregated ordering.
We believe that these revisions substantially improve both the readability of the figure and the interpretability of the numerical values presented in it.
Remark 7
The list of importance metrics is not provided. Apparently, that is why the article does not contain a summary of the research results, what the final research result looks like - customer Order Success Factors (conceptual) model.
Response 7
Thank you for this insightful comment. We agree that the manuscript should present both the feature-significance metrics and the final research result in a clear and integrated form. In the manuscript, the analytical framework identifies the five feature-significance evaluation methods used in the study (AUC, MI, dCor, LogReg, DecTree) and specifies their respective roles within the multi-algorithmic procedure.
Rather than adding a standalone conceptual model, we chose to strengthen the empirical synthesis already supported by the results. More specifically, the revised manuscript now presents the final research result through three connected levels: first, in the Results section, the aggregated ranking is summarized as several empirical groups of determinants (communication-related, customer-history, order-flexibility, financial, and temporal factors); second, in the Discussion section, these groups are interpreted in relation to prior studies and the observed cross-method patterns; third, in the Conclusions section, the findings are reformulated in a more structured tiered form that distinguishes the most stable and influential determinants, factors of secondary or method-dependent importance, and weak or negligible contributors.
We would also like to emphasize that the primary contribution of the study is the proposed multi-algorithmic methodological framework for feature ranking. The empirical ordering and grouping of factors reported in the manuscript are intended as an application-specific synthesis that demonstrates the applicability and interpretability of this framework in an ERP-based transactional setting, rather than as a universal conceptual model of B2B order success.
Thus, the revised manuscript now presents a clearer and more integrated summary of the final research result while remaining fully grounded in the empirical evidence generated by the multi-algorithmic analysis.
Remark 8
The conclusions are not systematized; positive and negative factors are not distinguished.
Response 8
Thank you for this remark. We agree that the Conclusions section benefits from a more systematic presentation. In the revised manuscript, we reorganized the final section to distinguish more clearly between the most stable and influential determinants of order success, factors of secondary or method-dependent importance, and variables with weak or negligible contribution in the consolidated ranking.
More specifically, the revised Conclusions now first identify the dominant factors (communication intensity and customer-history characteristics), then summarize factors of secondary or method-dependent importance (order flexibility and financial indicators), and finally separate discount-related and fine-grained temporal variables as the weakest contributors. We believe that this revised structure improves the logical coherence of the final section while also making the practical implications of the study clearer.
Once again, we sincerely thank you for your thoughtful and constructive review. We believe that the revisions made in response to your comments have significantly improved the clarity, methodological transparency, and overall quality of the manuscript.
With respect,
The Authors

## Response to Reviewer 2
Dear Reviewer,
We sincerely thank you for your careful reading of our manuscript, “Ranking B2B Order Success Factors in ERP Systems: A Multi-Algorithmic Approach,” and for your positive evaluation of its scientific and practical value. We highly appreciate your supportive comments and your recommendation regarding the publication of the manuscript.
Your review accurately reflects the main objective of our study, namely the identification and ranking of the factors associated with successful B2B order completion in an ERP-based transactional environment using a multi-algorithmic analytical framework. We are grateful for your recognition of the relevance of the topic, the appropriateness of the selected methodology, and the practical applicability of the obtained results.
In particular, we appreciate your emphasis on the following strengths of the manuscript: the practical importance of analyzing B2B order success in a real business environment; the use of a substantial real-world dataset covering transactions collected between 2017 and 2025; the justified application of five complementary feature-significance evaluation methods; the clear presentation of the results in tabular and graphical form; and the conclusion that communication-related factors, customer interaction history, and order flexibility play a central role in successful order completion.
We also thank you for drawing attention to the broader applicability of the proposed methodological approach beyond the specific empirical setting of the Odoo ERP system. We agree that the presented framework can be adapted to other domains in which transactional success depends on a combination of operational, historical, behavioral, and contextual factors. In such cases, the transferability of the approach depends primarily on two conditions: the appropriate redefinition of the target variable and the domain-specific construction of the feature space.
In the field of freight transportation by rail, the methodology could be adapted by redefining the target variable as successful delivery or successful execution of a transportation order. The corresponding feature space could include such factors as cargo type, route complexity, customs constraints, delivery distance, communication intensity with the client, prior cooperation history, schedule modifications, and infrastructure-related delays. Under these conditions, the proposed rank aggregation framework could help identify which operational and relational factors most strongly influence transportation success.
In the context of a large international pharmaceutical corporation, the same analytical logic could be transferred to pharmaceutical supply and distribution processes supported by several integrated ERP systems. Here, the target variable may correspond to the successful fulfillment of delivery orders to pharmacy chains or distributors, while the feature set may include product category, regulatory constraints, storage requirements, stock availability, urgency, customer history, communication factors, and supply chain disruptions. This multi-ERP scenario can be addressed by creating a unified data layer that normalizes comparable features across systems before applying the ranking procedure. The rank aggregation approach is particularly advantageous in such settings, because if different ERP systems capture partially different feature subsets, the modified Borda mean-rank procedure still enables a consistent consolidated ranking based on the available evidence.
Once again, we sincerely thank you for your constructive and encouraging review. Your comments confirm the relevance of the chosen research direction and support the practical significance of the proposed methodology. We highly value your attention to our work.
With respect,
The Authors

**Response 1.** We agree that the previous formulation did not state the contribution to the body of scientific knowledge with sufficient precision. - *Abstract*: rewritten to state a twofold contribution: (i) a method-neutral ranking method that compensates for individual feature-significance methods through cross-method triangulation, and (ii) an empirical taxonomy of B2B order success determinants in an ERP-based setting suitable for direct integration into ERP prediction modules.

### Comment 2
> Do not use different terms for the same object. For instance, methodology and approach are different terms. Similarly, method and algorithm have different meanings.

**Response 2.** We have introduced a strict, single-meaning-per-term convention throughout the manuscript, in line with standard usage in computer science and applied statistics: *method* (a defined procedure with specified input/output) is used both for the proposed contribution and for each of the five feature-significance methods (AUC, MI, dCor, LogReg, DecTree); *algorithm* is used only for concrete computational realizations (e.g., the CART algorithm underlying `DecisionTreeClassifier`; the Kraskov–Stögbauer–Grassberger algorithm for MI estimation); *technique* is reserved for auxiliary procedures (e.g., RobustScaler, OHE Extended Compact, modified Borda); *methodology* is no longer used as a synonym for the proposed contribution. The term *approach* is used only when explicitly referring to a higher-level strategy and is not interchanged with *method*.

**Changes in manuscript.**
- Replaced *methodology*/*approach* with *method* throughout Abstract, Sections 1, 3, 5, 6 wherever the referent is the proposed contribution.
- Renamed the five components consistently as *feature-significance methods* throughout Sections 1, 3, 4, 5, 6 and in Table 1 / Fig. 8.
- Reserved *algorithm* for explicit references to underlying computational realizations (Section 3 and the description of Fig. 7).

**Response 5.** We have retained the existing figures in their compact form to keep them legible at print size, and addressed the reviewer's concern by adding a stage-by-stage methodological description with concrete algorithms, libraries, and parameters in the running text accompanying each figure. **Changes in manuscript — under Fig. 6 (Section 3).**
- *Stage I (Feature engineering and removal of irrelevant variables):* described the transformation of the raw PostgreSQL export into the analytical dataset `cleanest_data.csv` (25 features), the three categories of removed variables — technical identifiers (`id`, `order_id`, `partner_id`, `customer_id`); low-predictive-value B2B attributes (`sales_team`, `customer_category`, `customer_country`, `product_categories`, `payment_term`, `delivery_method`); and post-event target-leakage variables (notably `processing_time_hours`) — the temporal-leakage-prevention rule for partner-level aggregates (current-order contribution subtracted from cumulative sums), and the near-zero-variance filter at the 95% majority-value threshold.
